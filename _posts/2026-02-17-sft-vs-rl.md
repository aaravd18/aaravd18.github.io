For the past few months, my team and I have been trying to teach an LLM to play
exploitative poker. More specifically, we are trying to train a 3 billion parameter model to leverage a solver (math-based poker tool) to play in a way that exploits its opponents biases. The goal is to make an AI that takes people's money.

It also turned out to be an instructive experience in understanding the role of supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning in modern post-training.

## First, a simple question

Before we could ask whether the model played poker well, we had to answer a more
basic question: could it play poker at all.

The model's outputs needed to follow a strict, solver-compatible format. Wrong format,
nothing downstream works. So the first phase of training was not about good decisions,
it was about usable decisions.

This is where supervised fine-tuning (SFT) comes in. SFT is fundamentally about teaching the model *how* to behave. This includes output format, reasoning style, and the overall shape of responses. After SFT, the model may not be optimal, but it does the thing. It plays poker, and it plays it well maybe one time in 100. That 1/100 is our diamond in the rough.

> A model must first exhibit a behavior before you can reinforce it.

## Now, a more interesting question

Once outputs were structurally correct, the problem changed. The question became: *is
this good poker?*

That is where RL joins the party. The format stays fixed, but the reward signal pushes the model toward better decisions. Now, how we quantify “better” is the [art of designing a reward signal](/posts/art-of-the-reward-function/). In poker, for example, “better” probably means decisions that win more chips in the long run.

## The mental model of post-training

When I was a kid, I thought of post-training as a choice between SFT and RL. 

Now that I'm a man, I know you use both. Modern post-training works because SFT and RL sequentially build on top of each other, not because one replaces the other.

**SFT defines the behavior space. RL optimizes within it.**

RL does not invent new behaviors. Instead, it shifts preferences among behaviors the model already knows how to produce. In essence, it answers the following question: given multiple valid responses, which ones should the model prefer?

## What we learned from DeekSeek

The DeepSeek approach to reasoning makes this concrete. They didn't throw RL at a blank
slate and wait for chains of thought to emerge. SFT first gave the model the form of
step-by-step reasoning, then RL taught the model to reason *well*.

Deepseek researchers recognized this themselves in their paper *DeepSeekMath: Pushing the Limits of Mathematical Reasoning in Open Language Models*:

> "RL enhances the model's overall performance by rendering the output distribution more
> robust ( ... ) it seems that the improvement is attributed to boosting the correct response
> from TopK rather than the enhancement of fundamental capabilities."
>
> — Shao et al., 2024

RL wasn't teaching the model new things. It was making the model more reliably pick the
right thing from what it already knew. The SFT foundation is doing more work than it
gets credit for.

## The actual pipeline

- **SFT** makes the model legible and controllable
- **RL** makes the model better at the objective

Skipping SFT and going straight to RL is like optimizing your workouts but you don't even go to the gym.

The reason modern post-training works is that these two stages are complementary. Not
competing, but sequential.
