## A 16MB Language Model on a $30 Computer

I pretrained a language model, squeezed it into 16MB, and got it running on a Raspberry Pi 3. In the video below we ran inference on the chip (on camera at the end) and streamed output to a little web server on my friend's laptop.

{% include video.html src="/videos/pi_inference.mp4" %}
<br>
The Pi has no GPU and cost us $30. The model generates about one token per second, and is dumb enough to be funny. But it's smart enough to be interesting and runs on a chip with less RAM than my Chrome browser tab.

## The Need For Compressed Intelligence

Most of the progress in deep learning over the last decade has come from the principle of making things bigger. Bigger models trained on bigger datasets served by bigger compute clusters. The frontier models that result are powerful, but they all run on someone else's data center.

Some of the most useful things a model could do are things you wouldn't want a server in the middle of. Like reading what's on your screen, or making a decision in the time it takes a car to stop. For those, the model has to be on the device, which means it has to be small.

## Parameter Golf

OpenAI must have found this question interesting too, because they outsourced it to the rest of us. Their [Parameter Golf](https://github.com/openai/parameter-golf) competition asks you to build the best possible language model in only 16MB. I based my run on [this entry](https://github.com/openai/parameter-golf/tree/main/records/track_10min_16mb/2026-04-06_SP8192_HessianSDClip_ProgressiveRecurrence) from the leaderboard, and my reproduction is in [my repo](https://github.com/aaravd18/golf-model). 

## Quantization, briefly

To fit maximum intelligence in 16MB the recipe uses several interesting tricks like depth recurrence and parallel residuals, but if I had to pick my favourite it would be the quantization scheme.

In naive quantization we uniformly reduce the precision with which we store each parameter (for example converting each parameter from a 16-bit floating point to an 8-bit floating point). One way of being smarter about this is a concept called Hessian-aware clipping. Put simply, weights whose perturbation barely affects the loss get quantized aggressively, and weights whose perturbation hurts the loss are treated more carefully. The Hessian tells you which is which. The result is a model that, on paper, has lost most of its precision, but in practice has lost it in the places that matter least.

## Final Thoughts

When you use a frontier model, the tokens come out faster than you can read them, and the experience is "the AI is fast." When you use a small model on a Pi, the tokens come out at the speed of thought, and the experience is "the computer is *thinking*." Slowly, and about nothing in particular. But thinking.
